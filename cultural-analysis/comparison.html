Comparison to Other Solo RPGs

Thousand Year Old Vampire

Thousand Year Old Vampire (Tim Hutchings, 2020) chronicles an immortal vampire’s centuries‑long life through numbered prompts and a memory‑management mechanic. Its introspective, often melancholic tone and forced choices about which memories to retain create a tightly woven narrative arc about loss and identity. This contrasts with Colostle’s sandbox‑style exploration and open‑ended journaling, where the player freely crafts adventures in a whimsical castle world. (Hutchings, 2020)

Journey

Journey (Luke Miller, 2022) is a worldbuilding solo RPG that guides players through creating their own fantasy settings via aspect‑based journaling prompts. Rather than leading a character through a plot, it helps writers flesh out cultures, histories, and magic systems. Like Colostle, it emphasizes creative freedom and narrative discovery, but its focus is on constructing the world itself rather than chronicling an adventurer’s exploits. (Miller, 2022)

Carta‑Based RPGs

Carta‑based solo RPGs (Peach Garden Games, 2022) use a shuffled deck laid out as a map grid. Players navigate by moving a token between cards, revealing new locations and responding to prompts, with a defined goal card marking the end. This structure adds spatial choice and a clear objective, blending narrative journaling with puzzle‑like progression. In contrast, Colostle offers freeform roaming through its endless rooms, prioritizing story prompts over mapped exploration. (Peach Garden Games, 2022)

Where Colostle Fits

Together, these titles showcase the diversity of solo RPGs: from the introspective memory puzzles of Thousand Year Old Vampire, to Journey’s worldbuilding workshop, to Carta’s mapped adventures. Colostle carves out its niche as a whimsical, journal‑first exploration game—offering both a vivid setting and open‑ended narrative freedom for the solo player.
