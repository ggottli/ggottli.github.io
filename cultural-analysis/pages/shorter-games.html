Shorter Games & Constant Engagement

How modern games respect your time and keep everyone involved

One of the most noticeable improvements in modern games is their pacing and engagement. The classic games of the past often dragged on for many hours or had long stretches where players simply waited for their turn. Games like Risk could take north of 8 hours depending on your play, an entire day's worth of work on one game. In contrast, newer games tend to be snappier and ensure that every turn is interesting. One example is a game like Carcassonne that usually takes around 30–45 minutes total. This is a far cry from sessions of Monopoly that could span the course of multiple days. This shorter playtime means players can actually finish a game in one sitting without losing steam, making the experience more satisfying.

Crucially, modern games also strive to eliminate the dull downtime that plagued older designs. In Pandemic, a popular cooperative game, each player’s turn involves important actions (treating diseases, traveling to new cities, strategizing with teammates) so that there’s always something meaningful to do. By comparison, in a classic like Clue, you might have turns where you simply can’t make any progress because you’re stuck moving toward a room, resulting in a whole turn “wasted.” This wastes time and taints the gameplay experience. Designers now recognize that having players sit idle is a quick way to lose fun. Thus, they craft games where every turn counts and each player remains mentally involved throughout. Since every turn is important, yours and the other players, everyone is constantly engaged and participating. This shift toward brevity and consistent excitement reflects a cultural preference for experiences that respect our time and keep us engaged.

It also is catering to the younger generation and their shortened attention span. Modern players are often adults with busy schedules or kids accustomed to fast-paced media. These everyday people are far less interested in four-hour slogs or waiting 15 minutes between turns. They want games that deliver fun gameplay in a reasonable amount of time. The popularity of quick but deep games (sometimes called “filler games” or “gateway games”) attests to this desire. Even traditionally longer game genres have been streamlined and some have come out with different versions geared towards quicker game lengths. Even games like Chess which has recently been mainstreamed during Covid has seen a huge rise in popularity. A big change associated with this is bullet (or rapid) chess where games can take less than a minute. Many strategy board games now aim for 60–90 minute playtimes where possible. By trimming the fat of boredom and endless length, contemporary board games ensure a more inclusive and fun experience for everyone at the table from start to finish.
