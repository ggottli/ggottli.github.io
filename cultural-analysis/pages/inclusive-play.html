Inclusive Play: The End of Player Elimination

Keeping everyone in the game from start to finish

Another hallmark of modern board game design is a focus on keeping all players involved until the end of the game. This seems like such a simple idea, but in many older competitive games, an unlucky/bad player could be eliminated early (bankrupt in Monopoly, wiped out in Risk, etc.). This player would then be forced to sit and watch while everyone else continued to play. This often led to boredom and hurt feelings for the ousted players. Today’s board games largely avoid strict player elimination. It’s now common for everybody to get to play for the entire duration, even if they’re behind in score. As one commentator noted, “In many modern board games, all players participate until the very end… That’s true for co-operative as well as competitive games”. This means no one has to watch the clock for an hour, feeling left out of the fun. Games have tried adapting to this new modern idea. Previous elimination games like Werewolf, have rebranded the narratology and rules to eliminate the elimination aspect. Newer Eurogames, like Catan, really helped introduce and popularize this idea in society.

The decline of player elimination ties into a broader social value of inclusivity and togetherness. Game designers recognize that people play board games to socialize and have a good time with friends or family. Eliminating someone part way through completely undermines that social experience. Instead, newer games find creative ways to keep everyone in the game. Some competitive games ensure that even if you’re losing, you still get to take actions each turn and can enjoy the process. Many games also employ comeback mechanics or scoring systems that keep the final outcome uncertain until the very end, so nobody feels “out” early. This also allows players who are behind to get back in it, keeping every round exciting. Even classic elimination style genres have evolved. For example, modern party games often have very short rounds or ways for eliminated players to rejoin in the next round, minimizing anyone’s downtime.

Another change is in some modern games even the losers get to accomplish something meaningful by the end of play. In an old war-game like Risk or a duel like Chess, the losing side is left with nothing. Their army gets destroyed or their pieces captured. But consider a popular modern game like Catan. Although it’s small and seems insignificant, even a player who comes in last will have built a couple of settlements and roads, contributing to the island’s development. They can look at the board and see something they created, however modest, instead of feeling like everything they did was wiped out. As the board game video pointed out, a losing player in Catan “will still have the settlement they placed down – it might not be as impressive as the winner’s, but it’s still something they’ve succeeded at building”. This design philosophy means no one walks away empty-handed. Psychologically, that’s a big improvement that science shows improves the overall experience of a game. It keeps players happier and more willing to play again, because the experience remains positive even in defeat. All these inclusive design choices show how modern board games emphasize engagement, fairness, and respect for all players, reflecting a social shift toward inclusive play where everyone has fun rather than a harsh focus on single winners and losers.
