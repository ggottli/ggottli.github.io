Accessibility & Inclusive Design

How modern board games are becoming more accessible to players of all abilities

The board game industry has made remarkable progress in recent years toward creating more accessible gaming experiences. Modern designers and publishers are increasingly prioritizing inclusive design practices that ensure everyone can enjoy the unique social and cognitive benefits of tabletop gaming.

Key Areas of Accessibility Innovation

Physical Accessibility

Larger cards and pieces, magnetic components, 3D-printed modifications, and ergonomic designs make games physically accessible.

Examples: Sagrada with color-blind friendly dice, Ticket to Ride with larger pieces

Visual Accessibility

High-contrast components, tactile elements, embossed cards, braille adaptations, and audio companion apps assist visually impaired players.

Examples: Nyctophobia, specially designed for blind and sighted players alike

Cognitive Accessibility

Clear rulebooks with visual examples, tutorial modes, scalable complexity, and digital learning aids help players of varying cognitive abilities.

Examples: Azul with simple mechanics but strategic depth

Beyond physical game components, accessibility extends to the cultural and financial aspects of the hobby. Publishers are increasingly offering print-and-play versions, app companions, and affordable editions to make gaming more financially accessible to diverse communities.

Industry Commitment

These efforts have created a virtuous cycle where market demand and designer awareness continually improve the accessibility landscape.

The rise of digital companion apps has further enhanced accessibility, providing automated setup instructions, rule clarifications, and even handling complex game-state management tasks that might otherwise present barriers to play.
