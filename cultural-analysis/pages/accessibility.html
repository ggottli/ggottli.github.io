Accessibility & Inclusive Design

How modern board games are becoming more accessible to players of all abilities

Modern board games have also become far more accessible and diverse, thanks to a number of design innovations that welcome all kinds of players. There is now truly “something for everyone” in the board gaming world. Family games used to dominate the board game space in the past, however today’s games come in a vast range of themes, complexities, and play styles. If you don’t enjoy Catan, perhaps you’d prefer the cooperative puzzle solving of Exit or the super popular party game Codenames. Are you unlucky and don’t enjoy heavy dice games? Try strategy games like the newer Azul. From war and combat to peaceful tile laying, word games, card games, and more, there are thousands of titles released each year. This variety means any group can likely find a game that suits their taste, whether it’s a quick and light 15-minute bluffing game or a long 4-hour strategy battle. This breadth of choice has made the hobby more inviting to people of all ages and backgrounds. In Western markets especially, the influx of European-style games since the 1990s showed players that board games can be about anything, not just roll-and-move around a track. Nowadays there’s a game out there suited for nearly any interest or hobby. An explosion of themes and mechanics took place and is a strong sign of inclusivity. More people can find a game that resonates with them, beyond the narrow offerings of the past.

Moreover, contemporary designers put huge effort into making games easy to learn and enjoyable to play for newcomers, without sacrificing depth for those who want it. They’ve struck the perfect balance of being “simple to teach, but difficult to master” and are living in that sweet spot. They often have well written rulebooks (that are still simple to learn usually), intuitive icons and imagery, and thoughtful tutorials or beginner setups to lower the barrier to entry. As one gaming essayist observes, if you want a game that “should suit most people,” it needs to have a low barrier to entry and ideally not take too long to play, because people are more willing to try something new if it’s short and sweet. This design philosophy has given rise to the so-called “gateway games” such as Ticket to Ride or Splendor that are very accessible to beginners yet still interesting enough for seasoned players. These act as entry points to the hobby, proving that you don’t need to slog through a 30-page manual (or more if you consider complex games like dungeons and dragons) to enjoy a board game. At the same time, many games include optional advanced rules or expansions that add complexity, essentially scaling to the level of the audience. Games like Catan have made a franchise off these expansions that allow elevated gameplay and more options for people wanting more out of a game. Game designers today also have to carefully tune the “weight” of a game to fit the masses. Too light and it may bore serious gamers, too heavy and it overwhelms casual players. Games often aim for a balance that allows different types of players to have fun together whether it's through rules, expansions, modules, or other features.

Beyond rules, modern board games excel in production quality and visual appeal, which is more than just eye candy but rather a way to draw people into the experience. Sturdy, attractive components and artwork can make a game far more inviting to play. Many of us remember the simple cardboard pawns and paper money of the old games. By contrast, today it’s common to find games with colorful custom tokens, miniatures, and beautifully illustrated boards. This tactile, visual allure is important in convincing new players to give a game a try. As one observer quipped, “we’ve all been guilty of stacking those nice wooden pieces or admiring a beautifully laid out game board – table presence matters!” Many games release new editions of these games updating the aesthetics, visuals, and pieces to meet the current consumer wants and needs. These games coming with thoughtful components not only make them more fun to play and enjoyable, but also help convey the theme and rules in an intuitive and better way.

Thanks to these innovations, the hobby is more inclusive than ever. It’s not just hardcore strategists who can find enjoyment, but families, casual players, youth and elders, friend groups, and even coworkers can all find games suited to them. There are games designed specifically for two players, games that accommodate big parties of ten or more, and solo games you can play by yourself. There are even educational and therapeutic board games tailored to teach concepts or build social skills. This democratization of game design shows a cultural commitment to making play universal. As one board game slogan goes, “not everyone will like every board game, but there is a board game for everyone”. That inclusive spirit marks a huge improvement from the days when a handful of similar mass-market games dominated every household. Modern board gaming celebrates diversity of game styles and of players and that’s a big part of why it’s flourishing.
